T-SNE and Reddit¶

Online forums provide wealth of language data and even, by the topical nature of the forums, come complete with some level of labeling. One can assume, with some level of confidence, that the language of a forum titled 'teenagers' has a strong relationship with the language of teenenagers themselves. Occaisonally, titles can be misleading; 'Superbowl' sometimes relates to America's National Football League and sometimes is connected with pictures of superb owls, but these sorts of issues are easily researched and relatively uncommon.

Researchers have already made some use of loosely labeled data to research patterns in the mental health community. The work below was inspired by Al-Mosaiwi and Johnstone's In an Absolute State which showed an increased use of absolutist words ('always', 'never', 'completely', etc) in forums concerning depression, anxiety, and suidical ideation. Al-Mosaiwi and Johnstone's work was limited by the nature of their dataset, which the curated by hand from forums found in google searches, but there exist larger collections and processing methods which could expand such inquiry to a larger domain.

Reddit.com represents truly substantial amount of forum data which has been generated and collected for more than 10 years. The data below represent a subset of reddit forums (called subreddits) and the text posts to those forums from a five year span (2012-2017). The subreddits were chosen somewhat arbitrarily to represent forums and topics which are particularly popular on reddit or of interest to mental health research. These topics and subreddits include:

  • Video gaming: leagueoflegends, gaming, DestinyTheGame, DotA2, ContestofChampions, StarWarsBattlefront, Overwatch, WWII, hearthstone, wow, heroesofthestorm, destiny2, darksouls3, fallout
  • Mental health: SuicideWatch, depression, OCD, dpdr, proED, Anxiety, BPD, socialanxiety, mentalhealth, ADHD, bipolar
  • Computers: buildapc, techsupport, buildapcforme, hacker, SuggestALaptop, hardwareswap, laptops, computers, pcmasterrace
  • Relationships: relationshps, relationship_advice, breakups, dating_advice, LongDistance, polyamory, wemetonline
  • Recreational drugs:MDMA, Drugs, trees, opiates, LSD
  • General: tifu, r4r, AskReddit, reddit.com, tipofmytongue, Life, Advice, jobs, teenagers, HomeImprovement, redditinreddit
  • Sports: FIFA, nba, hockey, nfl, mls, baseball
  • Media: BokuNoHeroAcademia, anime, movies, StrangerThings

One, computationally effecient way of representing language data is as a frequency of words belonging to one or more word categories. In tradiational sentiment analysis this might mean comparing the frequency of words from a positive emotional dictionary to the frequency from a negative emotional dictionary. Linguistic Inquiry and Worc Count (LIWC) dictionaries are a common set of ~60 word collections representing a large variety of topics. The dictionaries used to generate the data point below include (among others):

  • absolutist (as formulated by Al-Mosaiwi and Johnstone)
  • functional, pronoun, verb, past, present, future,
  • negate, number, swear, social, family,
  • positive emotion, negative emotion, anger, sad, tentative, certain,
  • body, ingest, motion, space, time,
  • work, achieve, leisure, home, money, death, filler

The remarkable result, is that each subreddit, represented by these dictionary freuencies alone, is completely distinguichable from other subreddits, and subreddits also tend to form clusers within the assigned categories. The easiest way to see this result, is via t-SNE dimensionality reduction which takes as input dictionary frequencies (or a normalized, PCA reduced representation thereof) and ouputs two dimensional points, which are graphed below. For more information on the t-SNE algorithm see the original paper on it or these slides introducing the concept (additional presentation notes to come).

The notebook code and graphs below present the data and resulting visualizations. I've removed the least interesting code blocks; for the curious, that code as well as a few other examples for other projects is available in this folder on my github.

One Month Visual¶

The following visualization is a scatter plot showing one month of data in which each point represents the t-SNE dimensionality reduction of numerical features representing one subreddit. The name of the subreddit associated with each point can be seen by hovering over the data point

Of note is the way, points representing each subreddit cluster with each other, with the exception of the 'general' category which includes the following, largely unrelated subreddits:

- tifu  (today I fucked up) 
- r4r
- AskReddit
- reddit.com
- tipofmytongue (in which people describe words they are searching for but can't remember)
- Life
- Advice
- jobs
- teenagers
- HomeImprovement
- redditinreddit

Also of interest is the way category clusters also form two larger groups, one containing the computers, video game, and sports categories and the other including forums about relationships, mental health, and drugs.

Five Year Visual¶

The following visualization is a scatter plot representing 5 years of data in which each point represents the t-SNE dimensionality reduction of numerical features representing one month of text posted to one subreddit. The name of the subreddit and the month represented can be seen by hovering over any data point. Note also that the bokeh toolbar to the right of the image should allow zooming.

While the categories are not so clearly delineated in this image as they are in the visualization of just one month of data (likely because the t-SNE algorithm struggles to represent global relations between so many points), relationships between months of data from the same subreddit are shockingly close. This suggests that each forum has its own linguistic habits that continue across time.
